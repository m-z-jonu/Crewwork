# F2: WhatsApp's Known Encryption Flaws

## Findings

### [1] WhatsApp does not protect metadata — Meta collects phone numbers, device info, IP addresses, contacts, and usage patterns despite E2EE
- quote: "it will allow Facebook to see data such as what phone and operating system a user has, the user's time zone, IP address, profile picture, status, phone number, app usage, and all of the contacts which are stored in WhatsApp"
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reception_and_criticism_of_WhatsApp_security_and_privacy_features
- source_type: secondary (aggregating primary sources including ProPublica investigation and 2024 FTC report)
- published: 2024-09 (FTC report section); 2021-01 (privacy policy update)
- confidence: high

### [2] WhatsApp uses the Signal Protocol but with a less private "Sender Keys" protocol for groups, and does not implement sealed sender — enabling server-side metadata correlation
- quote: "WhatsApp uses the Sender Keys protocol."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WhatsApp#End-to-end_encryption
- source_type: primary (technical documentation)
- published: ongoing (current as of 2026)
- confidence: high

### [3] WhatsApp cloud backups were not E2EE by default — opt-in encrypted backup was only added in October 2021, meaning years of backups stored in plaintext on iCloud/Google Drive
- quote: "On October 14, 2021, WhatsApp rolled out end-to-end encryption for backups on Android and iOS. The feature has to be turned on by the user and provides the option to encrypt the backup either with a password or a 64-digit encryption key."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WhatsApp#End-to-end_encryption
- source_type: primary (WhatsApp official feature documentation)
- published: 2021-10-14
- confidence: high

### [4] WhatsApp servers control group membership — a 2017 Ruhr University Bochum study found WhatsApp servers could add arbitrary phone numbers to group chats, making future communication insecure
- quote: "WhatsApp's servers effectively control the membership in groups. The report found that it would be therefore possible to add arbitrary phone numbers to a group chat such that future communication becomes insecure."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reception_and_criticism_of_WhatsApp_security_and_privacy_features
- source_type: secondary (reporting on peer-reviewed research from Ruhr University Bochum)
- published: 2017-01-15
- confidence: high

### [5] WhatsApp stores chat messages in an unencrypted SQLite database file "msgstore.db" on device, even when E2EE is active
- quote: "The application can store encrypted copies of the chat messages onto the SD card, but chat messages are also stored unencrypted in the SQLite database file 'msgstore.db'."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WhatsApp#End-to-end_encryption
- source_type: secondary
- published: unknown (documented in technical analysis)
- confidence: medium

### [6] WhatsApp's 2021 privacy policy update removed user opt-out for data sharing with Facebook, violating GDPR principles — EU was excluded to avoid penalties
- quote: "Previously, users could opt-out of such data sharing, but the new policy removed this option and required users to accept the policy in order to use the application... Due to GDPR regulations, WhatsApp had to exclude the European Union from this update to avoid penalties."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reception_and_criticism_of_WhatsApp_security_and_privacy_features
- source_type: secondary (reporting on regulatory/legal analysis)
- published: 2021-01
- confidence: high

### [7] WhatsApp shares metadata with law enforcement including real-time recipient data — ProPublica found ~1,000 contractors review reported messages and WhatsApp provided 400,000 NCMEC reports in 2020
- quote: "WhatsApp shares message metadata with law enforcement agencies such as the Department of Justice. If legally required, or at its own discretion... it can provide critical location or account information, or real-time data on the recipients messaged a target subject."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reception_and_criticism_of_WhatsApp_security_and_privacy_features
- source_type: secondary (ProPublica investigation, whistleblower SEC complaint)
- published: 2021-09
- confidence: high

### [8] WhatsApp failed to implement E2EE consistently across platforms — iOS messages were not end-to-end encrypted until April 2016 despite Android-to-Android encryption starting in late 2014
- quote: "In April 2015, German magazine Heise security used ARP spoofing to confirm that the protocol had been implemented for Android-to-Android messages, and that WhatsApp messages from or to iPhones running iOS were still not end-to-end encrypted."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WhatsApp#End-to-end_encryption
- source_type: secondary (Heise Security technical verification)
- published: 2015-04
- confidence: high

### [9] WhatsApp channels (broadcast feature launched 2023) are NOT end-to-end encrypted
- quote: "Unlike messages in groups or private chats, channels are not end-to-end encrypted."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WhatsApp#Communities_and_Channels
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2023-06
- confidence: high

### [10] European Commission fined Facebook €110 million in 2017 for falsely claiming it was technically impossible to combine WhatsApp and Facebook user data during the 2014 acquisition
- quote: "The European Commission announced that it was fining Facebook €110 million for 'providing misleading information about WhatsApp takeover' in 2014. The Commission said that in 2014 when Facebook acquired the messaging app, it 'falsely claimed it was technically impossible to automatically combine user information from Facebook and WhatsApp.'"
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WhatsApp
- source_type: primary (European Commission official ruling)
- published: 2017-05-18
- confidence: high

### [11] WhatsApp uploads full phone address book including non-WhatsApp users' phone numbers to its servers — earlier used unsalted MD5 hashes, only fixed after Canadian-Dutch investigation
- quote: "WhatsApp required users to upload their mobile phone's entire address book, including contact information for contacts who were not using WhatsApp, to be mirrored on WhatsApp's servers... the data was not salted."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reception_and_criticism_of_WhatsApp_security_and_privacy_features
- source_type: secondary (Canadian-Dutch government investigation)
- published: 2013-01 (investigation); 2015 (hashing fix)
- confidence: high

### [12] WhatsApp co-founder Jan Koum left in 2018 citing privacy concerns; co-founder Brian Acton stated "I sold my users' privacy" regarding Meta's data practices
- quote: "Jan Koum announces he's leaving the company after clashes with Facebook, saying 'I sold my users' privacy'."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reception_and_criticism_of_WhatsApp_security_and_privacy_features
- source_type: primary (direct quotes from founders)
- published: 2018-04
- confidence: high
